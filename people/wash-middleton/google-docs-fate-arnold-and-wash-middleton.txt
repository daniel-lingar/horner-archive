The story of "Fate" Arnold and Wash Middleton is a grim account of the Unionist counter-insurgency in the Arkansas Ozarks during the Civil War. Their actions were part of a specialized squad led by Dr. John Turner Horner, formed specifically to eliminate Confederate "bushwhackers" following a personal tragedy.

The Catalyst: The Murder of Spencer Horner
In September 1864, a band of roughly 20 Confederate irregulars (bushwhackers) raided the farm of Spencer Horner in Johnson County, Arkansas.
- Spencer Horner was shot and killed in his potato patch after attempting to surrender.
- William Horner, Spencer's son, was shot in the head while fleeing; he reportedly survived for several days hiding in a cave before succumbing to his wounds.
- The raiders were reportedly motivated by a search for money hidden on the farm.

NOTE: Date should read August 1864 per tombstone and 1942 transcript — September is an error in this Google Doc draft.

The Horner Squad
In retaliation, Spencer's other son, Dr. John Turner Horner, organized a squad of "desperate men" to hunt down the killers.
- Fate Arnold: Described as a man who "didn't know what fear was."
- Wash Middleton: Known for his brutal efficiency.

Key Encounters:
- Cornfield skirmish — each man picked his target; Dr. Horner threw his pistol when his gun failed.
- Dance raid — Wash Middleton dragged Spencer's killer out by the hair: "Now you'll kill a helpless old man."
- Success rate — 17 of 20 bushwhackers killed (oral history).

Goodspeed excerpt (Barry County, 1888, p. 1030) included in same export:
"In September, 1864, he was murdered on his farm by bushwhackers for his money. His son, William R., was murdered the same day on Mulberry Creek in the county."
