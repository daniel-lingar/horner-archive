## 4. Horner reprisal squad — family oral history (1942)

**Source:** Eva Horner family history, recorded by Eva from Prof. John Turner Horner (second-generation oral tradition). Preserved FindAGrave #9297073. Quoted on site: `transcript-1942.html`, §07.

**Squad named:** Dr. John Turner Horner (leader), **Wash Middleton**, Fate Arnold, Jess Wilson.

### Verbatim Middleton passages

> "Dad had a squad of men, including **Wash Middleton** and Fate Arnold, desperate men, to go back into the hills and clean out that bunch. They killed 17 out of the 20 bushwhackers."

> **Road killing:** "They caught one man walking between his wife and his mother. **Wash Middleton shot him down.**"

> **Dance house raid:** "One night they surrounded a house where the bushwhackers were having a dance. **Wash Middleton took the man who killed grandfather by the hair and dragged him out**, 'Now you'll kill a helpless old man' and killed him."

### Archive interpretation (§07)

- Squad described as Union veterans with local terrain knowledge operating after August 1864 attack.
- **17 of 20** body count — **sole source is 1942 transcript**; no provost, court, or newspaper confirmation located.
- Middleton credited with killing the man who murdered **John Spencer Horner** (potato patch, 2 Aug 1864).
- Rolling Stone / Lingar narrative (2026) adds: dragged a man from beneath women's skirts (may conflate Jess Wilson incident with Middleton — treat as narrative synthesis until primary split).

## 5. Wash Gibbs — the fictional character made from Wash Middleton

**User-confirmed thesis (project standard):** The character **Wash Gibbs** in Harold Bell Wright's Ozarks novel was built from the life and legend of **George Washington "Wash" Middleton** — especially his physical presence, Civil War/Bald Knobber violence, and regional reputation as a man who enforced justice outside the law.

This is **not** primary evidence for the 1864 Horner reprisal, but it is **documented regional tradition** and matters for how Middleton's story survived in public memory.

### 5.1 The novel and author

| Item | Detail |
|------|--------|
| Title | ***The Shepherd of the Hills*** |
| Author | **Harold Bell Wright** (1872–1944) — minister, novelist, outdoorsman |
| Published | **1907** (written in Lebanon, MO, from Ozark notes 1896–1904) |
| Setting | **Mutton Hollow / Roark Valley** — Taney & Stone County, Missouri Ozarks (Branson corridor) |
| Wright's method | Listened to local children; "twenty characters to make one" (his own statement, *Bittersweet* 1977) |

### 5.2 Who Wash Gibbs is in fiction

In the novel, **Wash Gibbs** is the violent antagonist/regulator figure of the hill country — a large, dangerous man whose power terrifies the hollow. He embodies Ozark vigilantism and personal feud law, not courthouse law. He is **not** a documentary portrait of one man; Wright composite characters from many locals.

### 5.3 Middleton → Gibbs: best source on file

**Shepherd of the Hills Farm research**, reported in *Bittersweet* (Springfield-Greene County Library, **Summer 1977**, "From Hills to Hotels"):

> The research of the people from the Shepherd of the Hills Farm says [Wash Gibbs] may be based on two men. **The first, Wash Middleton**, lived across Roark Valley by the Union Pacific Railroad. He was a **tall, ruggedly built and burley-looking man** who possessed a **very gentle personality**. The second Gibbs, a man by the name of **May** who rode with the Bald Knobbers, was quite different. **It is felt that Wright combined Middleton's physical characteristics and May's personality** to make Wash Gibbs as he appears in *The Shepherd of the Hills*.

**How to read this source:**

| Element | Middleton contribution | May contribution |
|---------|------------------------|------------------|
| Body | Tall, burly, rugged | — |
| Demeanor in novel | — | Bald Knobber violence / menace |
| Geography | Roark Valley (same Ozark theater as Horner reprisal network) | Taney County Bald Knobber period |

**Correction:** Bittersweet describes Middleton as "gentle" in person — the **fictional Gibbs is not gentle**. The dossier treats Gibbs as a **composite**: Middleton's frame + May's ruthlessness.

### 5.4 Parallels between Middleton life and Gibbs legend

| Real Middleton (documented / oral) | Gibbs / regional lore |
|-----------------------------------|------------------------|
| Union 1st Lt., Co. E, 2nd Arkansas Infantry (US) | Hill-country enforcer outside formal law |
| 1942 Horner transcript — dance-house killing, road killing | Summary justice, public naming of crimes |
| Bald Knobber subordinate of Nat Kinney | Ozark vigilante milieu Wright dramatized |
| Snapp killing (1886) — called a **bushwhacker** | Civil War guerrilla vocabulary still alive in 1880s |
| Shot down at Parthenon picnic (1888) | Died in boots; "iron nerve and dead shot" (*Mountaineer*) |
| Unmarked grave, Buffalo Cemetery | No tombstone accusation like William Riley Horner's — silence vs. stone |

### 5.5 Film and stage adaptations

| Version | Notes |
|---------|-------|
| **1941 Paramount film** | **Ward Bond** plays **Wash Gibbs**; **John Wayne** plays **Young Matt Matthews** (lead), **not** Gibbs |
| Earlier Hollywood version | Filmed in California; locals rejected it (*Bittersweet* — Ward Bond as Old Matt in that account; versions differ by release) |
| Branson tourist play | Nightly performance at Shepherd of the Hills Farm on the actual landscape |
| Ancestry gallery | Photo caption: "George Washington Middleton — Wash Gibbs in the book Shepherd of the Hills" |

**Do not repeat the error** that John Wayne played Wash Gibbs. Wayne hunted Gibbs as Young Matt in the 1941 film.

### 5.6 Source tiers for the Middleton = Gibbs claim

| Tier | Source |
|------|--------|
| **Regional secondary (strong)** | *Bittersweet* / Shepherd of the Hills Farm research, 1977 — names Middleton first |
| **Family / genealogical** | Ancestry life-story gallery (`horners.pdf`); dossier v2 §XIX |
| **Project narrative** | Rolling Stone Lingar profile (June 2026) — literary afterlife paragraph |
| **Not yet obtained** | Wright letters, publisher correspondence, or Wright autobiography stating Middleton by name |

**OQ status:** The Bittersweet passage **partially closes** the "who was Gibbs?" question. A Wright primary document naming Middleton would fully close it.

### 5.7 Public-history wording (for site and dossier)

> Wash Gibbs is a fictional character. Regional research and family tradition hold that Harold Bell Wright modeled him in part on **George Washington "Wash" Middleton** — the same Union officer named in the 1942 Horner reprisal transcript — combining Middleton's physical presence with the violent reputation of Bald Knobber-era men in the Missouri–Arkansas Ozarks.

See also: `wash-gibbs-shepherd-of-the-hills.md` in this folder.

## 6. Postwar — Bald Knobbers and later violence (1886–1888)

Secondary but well-sourced regional history (*Wash and Jim*, Newton County AR blog, Jan 2024 — draws on Vincent Anderson, *Bald Knobbers: Chronicles of Vigilante Justice*).

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| Post-1865 | Middleton associated with **Bald Knobber** vigilantes, Taney County, Missouri (former Union Capt. Nathan "Nat" Kinney) |
| **28 Feb 1886** | Kinney kills Andrew Coggburn at Oak Grove Church; **Sam Snapp** witness |
| **9 May 1886** | Shoots and kills **Samuel H. Snapp** at Kirbyville; Snapp called him **bushwhacker**; **3 shots**; accomplice **Alonzo Prather** (dossier v2); coroner: willful murder (*St. Louis Globe-Democrat*, 13 May 1886) |
| 1886 | Tried; sentence reduced petition from 45 to **15 years** |
| Night ~1886 | **Jailbreak** at Forsyth — sons + Bald Knobbers + Sheriff J.K. McHaffie |
| Hiding | Brother's home, **Parthenon**, Newton County hills |
| **4 Jul 1888** | Masonic picnic, Parthenon (~250 people). Middleton armed (3 pistols + Winchester) with sons. **U.S. Detective J.L. Holt** (disguised as tramp) shoots Middleton below right eye at ~10 feet; dies instantly. Sons disarmed by Sheriff John Lee / deputies |

**1888 death variants on file:**

- Shot by **Jim Holt**, private detective / bounty hunter, after escape from Taney County jail (FindAGrave note, Tom Todd).
- Convicted of **second-degree murder** before escape (same note).

**Newspaper tone:** "Man of iron nerve and a dead shot… died in his boots" (*The Mountaineer*, 27 Jul 1888, via Newton County blog).
