## Why this is not "just a deed search"

Family oral history (1942 transcript) says Permelia Turner Horner **sold the farm for a horse and wagon** after Spencer's murder. That story points researchers toward Johnson County **deed books 1865–1870**. Those books must be searched — but the **probate estate files** are the higher-probability front door.

When a man died intestate or with unresolved property in 1864 Arkansas, the **estate administration** often records:

- Real and personal property inventory (compare to 1860 census: $200 real / $1,000 personal on John T. "Homer" line)
- Administrator's bond and court orders
- **Creditor claims** and **court-ordered sales** of land
- Heir petitions, guardian appointments, and notices to out-of-county heirs
- The **grantee name** if land passed by administrator's deed rather than widow's voluntary sale

A Permelia deed may exist. It may also be **derivative of estate papers** filed under Spencer's name, not hers.
