1942 Horner Family Transcript — Excerpts

Recorded by Eva Horner from Prof. John Turner Horner · FindAGrave Memorial #9297073
Archive Home The Attack The Reckoning Sources

How to read this page. These passages are excerpted from a single known copy of the 1942 Horner family history, preserved in FindAGrave Memorial #9297073. They are second-generation oral tradition — Prof. John Turner Horner (son of Dr. John Turner Horner) telling his daughter Eva what his father told him. They are indispensable to this archive but are not court records, muster rolls, or land patents. Where the transcript conflicts with primary documents, both readings are kept.

Provenance

  • Recorder: Eva Horner, 1942 (78 years after August 1864)
  • Speaker chain: Dr. John Turner Horner → Prof. John Turner Horner → Eva Horner → written transcript
  • Preservation: FindAGrave #9297073 (privately held copy cited by researcher)
  • Corroboration: William Riley Horner tombstone (Aug 10, 1864); Union muster roll; GPS site survey (2003); FamilySearch LH23-VP3

August 2, 1864 — Warning and Attack

Oral history · 1942 transcript
"Dad said his horse needed shoeing and he had to go out on the mountain 5 miles to get it shod. He told his brother, Will, and his father Spencer Horner that whatever they did, never go to the house in the daytime. But the mother was killing a chicken for dinner, and the house was where the food was."
— 1942 Eva Horner transcript, via Prof. John Turner Horner
"Wasn't long till about 20 rebels came riding up. Grandfather went out into the potato patch and said, 'I surrender!' and they shot him down."
— 1942 Eva Horner transcript
"Will ran through the lot and up on the mountain side and was shot in the head."
— 1942 Eva Horner transcript

Documented William Riley Horner's tombstone fixes the death date as August 10, 1864 and names the killers as parties known to the family. The Union muster roll reads: "reported killed by guerillas 10 August, 1864, Johnson Co., Arkansas."

The Hidden Gold

Oral history · 1942 transcript
"Grandfather had a pot of gold and silver and paper money, but they never did find it after he was killed. Louis had a dream about 'a black gum tree in the middle of a field — put my back to the tree, took ten steps toward the house, dug down, and found the pot of gold.'"
— 1942 Eva Horner transcript

The Reprisal Hunt

Oral history · sole source for body count
"Dad had a squad of men, including Wash Middleton and Fate Arnold, desperate men, to go back into the hills and clean out that bunch. They killed 17 out of the 20 bushwhackers."
— 1942 Eva Horner transcript · No military or court record confirms this count
Cornfield ambush: "One day they met in a corn field and each man picked out his man. Dad picked out the leader; couldn't shoot him, threw his pistol at him."
— 1942 Eva Horner transcript
Road killing: "They caught one man walking between his wife and his mother. Wash Middleton shot him down."
— 1942 Eva Horner transcript
Dance house raid: "One night they surrounded a house where the bushwhackers were having a dance. Wash Middleton took the man who killed grandfather by the hair and dragged him out, 'Now you'll kill a helpless old man' and killed him."
— 1942 Eva Horner transcript · Also quoted in main archive §07

Squad Members — Additional Detail

"Dad said Jess Wilson was a brave man too. One time they left Jess Wilson to hold their horses when they went to fight. Jess was another little measley, looked like he wouldn't fight a chicken."
— 1942 Eva Horner transcript
"Fate Arnold would volunteer to go through 20 miles of Rebel lines to get ammunition, and he'd always come back. Once he jerked his horse's head up, and the horse got shot in the head. Arnold got wounded. He stayed with my father in Marshfield, Missouri until he got well. Mother said there'd be a pool of blood under his chair."
— 1942 Eva Horner transcript
Years later, meeting Fate Arnold's son-in-law in Stone County: "He was a little wizened old man."
— 1942 Eva Horner transcript

Known Conflicts With Primary Records

  • Dr. John Turner Horner's service: The 1942 account describes Confederate conscription and desertion; FindAGrave biographical notes and rejected pension records support only a brief 10th Arkansas Militia muster (Feb–Mar 1862) and dispute formal Union enlistment. Both readings are documented in the main archive.
  • 17 of 20 killed: No provost marshal, tribunal, or civil docket has been found. The claim rests on this transcript alone.
  • Attack location: Some FamilySearch contributor notes place Spencer in the yard; the 1942 transcript and HeatherAcord notes emphasize the potato patch. Both are noted in §01.
